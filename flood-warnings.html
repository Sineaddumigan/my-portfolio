Delivering an enhanced flood warning service to build a more resilient nation

The Warning Improvement Project (WIP) aims to deliver an improved flood warning service by 2025. The existing system, largely unchanged since the UK Government's initial investment in 2006, is being reimagined to better prepare and inform the public. The project’s mission is to create a resilient, 24/7/365 flood warning capability that’s future-ready and inspires effective action against flooding.

Flooding is a significant risk in England, with approximately 5 million homes and businesses at risk, and 4.7% of land classified as high-risk flood zones. As a ‘Category 1’ responder, the Environment Agency (EA) has a legal obligation to provide timely, accurate, and actionable flood warnings to safeguard people and property.

Understanding Existing Issues

The current system is divided into two services: one for citizens and one for organizations. While citizens typically upload only a handful of locations, organizations may manage up to 30,000 locations, introducing significant challenges.

Key Issues Identified:

  • Technical Complexity: The existing system requires GIS skills, accepting only X and Y coordinates or shapefiles, limiting accessibility for non-technical users.
  • Postcode Challenges: New postcodes may take months to appear in Ordnance Survey’s database, creating discrepancies in data recognition.
  • Volume of data: Organizations frequently upload between 2,000 to 30,000 locations, necessitating robust data handling mechanisms.
  • Flood Risk Understanding:
    • Organizations often lack clarity on the flood risk associated with their locations or whether warning services are available.
    • Some high-risk addresses fall outside warning/alert zones, as highlighted in a Floodline report showing 3,997 high-risk addresses with no warning service and 23,366 medium-risk addresses with no warning service.
    • Users may also struggle to understand the implications of their alert settings, risking overwhelm from excessive alerts.



Solutions & outcomes

To address the diverse needs of users, we developed flexible solutions for data uploads and flood warning configurations:

Simplifying Location Uploads

We provided multiple options to accommodate users with varying skill levels:

  • Upload by address or postcode using spreadsheets.
  • Upload by X and Y coordinates for GIS-skilled users.
  • Upload via shapefiles for advanced mapping needs.
  • Manual entry for minimal data.
Each option offers a tailored journey, with clear, step-by-step instructions.

Handling Partial Matches

For cases where location data partially matches system records:

  • Users can edit the address directly within the interface.
  • If unresolved, unmatched locations are moved to a downloadable spreadsheet for further review and reupload.
Each option offers a tailored journey, with clear, step-by-step instructions.

Setting Up Flood Warnings Effectively

To support organizations in configuring warnings:

  • We presented two primary options upfront:
    1. Recommended Setup:
      • Displays the number of warnings issued for selected locations over the past two years.
      • Includes detailed guidance on warning types and their implications.
    2. Custom Setup:
      • Allows users to tailor warnings to their specific needs, with additional granularity.
  • Before users choose their warnings, the system provides clear explanations of the different warning types, helping users make informed decisions.

Conclusion

This project tackled complex technical and user-centric challenges to modernize the flood warning service. By designing intuitive and flexible solutions, we ensured that the service accommodates a diverse range of users, from GIS experts to housing associations. The groundwork laid during this phase ensures a clear, user-centered direction for future development, aligning with the project’s mission of building a resilient, informed nation ready to respond to flooding.
