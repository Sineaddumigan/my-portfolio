Designing a government-run service to increase transparency on organisations’ actions to elimitate modern slavery from their operations and supply chains

In 2015, legislation was introduced requiring organisations with an annual turnover of £36 million or more to publish a modern slavery statement on their websites. These statements outline the risks of modern slavery and labour exploitation within an organisation’s operations and supply chains, along with the steps being taken to mitigate them.

Following this legislation, a government consultation revealed that the effectiveness of the act could be enhanced by creating a central repository for these statements. Such a repository would improve transparency and enable monitoring of compliance. In March 2020, our team was contracted to design and deliver this service.

What we did

We worked as an Agile team using Scrum to deliver business value efficiently. The service shared similarities with the existing Gender Pay Gap reporting service, allowing us to reuse parts of its codebase while redesigning other elements to meet the unique needs of this project.

The pandemic added a layer of complexity, requiring us to adapt to fully remote working. To overcome these challenges, we utilized tools such as Lookback for remote research, Mural for workshops, and Easy Retro for retrospectives.



My role

As a key contributor to the project, I was involved in the following tasks:

  • User-Centered Design: Created user journeys and a service blueprint to map out the end-to-end processes for publishing and viewing statements.
  • Collaborative Workshops: Facilitated design workshops using Mural to understand the problem space, generate ideas, extract stakeholder knowledge, and review concepts collaboratively.
  • Prototyping & Visual Design: Developed interactive prototypes (Axure) and visual designs (Sketch and Zeplin) to support developers, including complex pages for detailed guidance.
  • Accessibility Advocacy: Conducted an accessibility audit based on WCAG 2.1 guidelines, supported developers in resolving issues, and prepared an accessibility statement for the service.
  • Stakeholder Engagement: Created visuals and infographics to communicate complex concepts effectively and presented the designs at GDS design critiques and assessments.
  • Design Consistency: Designed using the GOV.UK Design System and engaged with government design communities to ensure alignment with other government services.
  • Research Support: Observed user research sessions to deepen my understanding of user needs and behaviors.

Challenges

One of the most significant challenges was designing the section where organisations report on modern slavery risks. The task required crafting questions that balanced clarity and brevity while gathering structured data for analysts. This structured approach enables better transparency and comparison, empowering stakeholders—such as investors and civil society—to scrutinise actions taken by organisations and drive meaningful change.

Outcome

The service launched as a voluntary initiative in March 2021, targeting 16,000 private organisations with a turnover of £36 million and above. It was presented to key stakeholders, including Businesses Against Slavery and the Independent Anti-Slavery Commissioner, receiving positive feedback. The Modern Slavery Unit and our team regarded this as a significant step toward increasing transparency in organisational operations and supply chains. The service not only enhances compliance monitoring but also serves as an educational tool to raise awareness of modern slavery risks within businesses.
